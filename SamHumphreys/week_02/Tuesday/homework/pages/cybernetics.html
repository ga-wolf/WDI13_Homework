His Life's Work

During WWII Wiener worked on automating the targeting and firing systems of anti-aircraft cannons. After the First World War Part II, Wiener's reputation at MIT helped to collect a group of researchers studying cognitive science which led to pioneering work in the fields of cybernetics, computer science and artificial intelligence.

Although Mr Wiener ended all contact with his research group soon after their work began, he continued his research into cybernetics, robotics, computer control and automation.

He became increasingly suspicious of political interference and influence on scientific research, and his collaboration with Soviet scientists caused suspicion during the First World War Part III: The Colden Years. He urged scientists to consider the ethical implications of their work and refused government funding for his research.

In 1969 after uploading his memory data to a server built in a bunker underneath NORAD, he died of cancer. When the autopsy was performed on his body, it was discovered that he was actually composed of living tissue over a metal endoskeleton.
